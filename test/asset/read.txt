dotted">s</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ɜr/ 'ir' in 'bird'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ɜr</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'b' in 'buy'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ər</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ə/ 'u' in 'syrup'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ə</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'" style="border-bottom:1px language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Κέρβερος</span> <i>Kerberos</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Greek" title="Help:IPA for Greek">[ˈkerberos]</a></span>) in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman mythology</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Polycephaly" title="Polycephaly">multi-headed</a> (usually three-headed) dog, or "<a href="/wiki/Hellhound" title="Hellhound">hellhound</a>" <sup id="cite_ref-m-w_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-m-w-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> with a serpent's tail, a mane of snakes, and lion's claws.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> He guards the entrance of <a href="/wiki/The_underworld" title="The underworld" class="mw-redirect">the underworld</a> to prevent the dead from escaping and the living from entering. Cerberus is featured in many works of ancient Greek and Roman literature and in works of both ancient and modern art and architecture, although the depiction of Cerberus differs across various renditions. The most notable difference is the number of his heads: Most sources describe or depict three heads; others show Cerberus with two or even just one; a smaller number of sources show a variable number, sometimes as many as fifty or even a hundred.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_myth">In myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerberus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cerberus is the offspring of <a href="/wiki/Echidna_(mythology)" title="Echidna (mythology)">Echidna</a>, a hybrid half-woman and half-serpent, and <a href="/wiki/Typhon" title="Typhon">Typhon</a>, a gigantic monster even the Greek gods feared. Its siblings are the <a href="/wiki/Lernaean_Hydra" title="Lernaean Hydra">Lernaean Hydra</a>; <a href="/wiki/Orthrus" title="Orthrus">Orthrus</a>, a two-headed <a href="/wiki/Hellhound" title="Hellhound">hellhound</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Chimera_(mythology)" title="Chimera (mythology)">Chimera</a>, a three-headed monster.<sup id="cite_ref-perseus-cerberustask_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perseus-cerberustask-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The common depiction of Cerberus in Greek mythology and art is as having three heads. In most works, the three heads respectively see and represent the past, the present, and the future, while other sources suggest the heads represent birth, youth, and old age.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Each of Cerberus' heads is said to have an appetite only for live meat and thus allow only the spirits of the dead to freely enter the underworld, but allow none to leave.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Cerberus was always employed as Hades' loyal watchdog, and guarded the gates that granted access and exit to the underworld.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Twelfth_Labour_of_Heracles">The Twelfth Labour of Heracles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerberus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Twelfth Labour of Heracles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Capturing Cerberus, without using weapons, was the <a href="/wiki/Labours_of_Hercules" title="Labours of Hercules">final labour</a> assigned to <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a> (Hercules) by <a href="/wiki/Eurystheus" title="Eurystheus">King Eurystheus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> in recompense for the killing of his own children by <a href="/wiki/Megara_(mythology)" title="Megara (mythology)">Megara</a> after he was driven insane by <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a>, and therefore was the most dangerous and difficult.</p>
<p>After having been given the task, Heracles went to <a href="/wiki/Eleusis" title="Eleusis">Eleusis</a> to be initiated in the <a href="/wiki/Eleusinian_Mysteries" title="Eleusinian Mysteries">Eleusinian Mysteries</a> so he could learn how to enter and exit the underworld alive, and in passing absolve himself for killing centaurs. He found the entrance to the underworld at <a href="/wiki/Tanaerum" title="Tanaerum" class="mw-redirect">Tanaerum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hermes" title="Hermes">Hermes</a> helped him to traverse the entrance in each direction. He passed <a href="/wiki/Charon_(mythology)" title="Charon (mythology)">Charon</a> with Hestia's assistance and his own heavy and fierce frowning.</p>
<p>Whilst in the underworld, Heracles met <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pirithous" title="Pirithous">Pirithous</a>. The two companions had been imprisoned by Hades for attempting to kidnap <a href="/wiki/Persephone" title="Persephone">Persephone</a>. One tradition tells of snakes coiling around their legs then turning into stone; another tells that Hades feigned hospitality and prepared a feast inviting them to sit. They unknowingly sat in chairs of forgetfulness and were permanently ensnared. When Heracles had pulled Theseus first from his chair, some of his thigh stuck to it (this explains the supposedly lean thighs of Athenians), but the earth shook at the attempt to liberate Pirithous, whose desire to have the wife of a god for himself was so insulting, he was doomed to stay behind.</p>
<p>Heracles found <a href="/wiki/Hades" title="Hades">Hades</a> and asked permission to bring Cerberus to the surface, to which Hades agreed if Heracles could overpower the beast without using weapons. Heracles was able to overpower Cerberus and proceeded to sling the beast over his back, dragging it out of the underworld through a cavern entrance in the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a> and bringing it to Eurystheus.</p>

<p>Cerberus featured in many prominent works of Greek and Roman literature, most famously in <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Peisandros_of_Rhodes" title="Peisandros of Rhodes" class="mw-redirect">Peisandros of Rhodes</a>' <a href="/wiki/Epic_poem" title="Epic poem" class="mw-redirect">epic poem</a> the <i><a href="/wiki/Labours_of_Hercules" title="Labours of Hercules">Labours of Hercules</a></i>, the story of <a href="/wiki/Orpheus" title="Orpheus">Orpheus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s <i>Symposium</i>, and in <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>, which is the only known reference to one of Heracles' labours which first appeared in a literary source.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The depiction of Cerberus is relatively consistent between different works and authors, the common theme of the mane of serpents is kept across works, as is the serpent's tail, most literary works of the era describe Cerberus as having three heads with the only notable exception being <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Theogony" title="Theogony">Theogony</a></i> in which he had 50 heads.<sup id="cite_ref-hesiod-differs_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hesiod-differs-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Most occurrences in ancient literature revolve around the basis of the threat of Cerberus being overcome to allow a living being access to the underworld; in the <i>Aeneid</i> Cerberus was lulled to sleep after being tricked into eating drugged honeycakes and Orpheus put the creature to sleep with his music. Capturing Cerberus alive was the twelfth and final labour of Heracles. In <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Inferno_(Dante)" title="Inferno (Dante)">Inferno</a></i>, Canto VI, the "great worm" Cerberus is found in the Third Circle of Hell, where he oversees and rends to pieces those who have succumbed to <a href="/wiki/Gluttony" title="Gluttony">gluttony</a>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_deadly_sins" title="Seven deadly sins">seven deadly sins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Cerberus_(constellation)" title="Cerberus (constellation)">constellation Cerberus</a> introduced by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Hevelius" title="Johannes Hevelius">Johannes Hevelius</a> in 1687, Cerberus is sometimes substituted for the "branch from the tree of the golden apples" fetched by Atlas from the garden of the Hesperides.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art">In art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerberus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:12th_labour_of_Heracles_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19119.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/12th_labour_of_Heracles_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19119.png/220px-12th_labour_of_Heracles_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19119.png" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/12th_labour_of_Heracles_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19119.png/330px-12th_labour_of_Heracles_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19119.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/12th_labour_of_Heracles_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19119.png/440px-12th_labour_of_Heracles_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19119.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="376" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

In this vase painting, Heracles leads a two-headed Cerberus out of Hades.</div>

</div>
<p>Numerous references to Cerberus have appeared in ancient <a href="/wiki/Greek_art" title="Greek art">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_art" title="Roman art">Roman art</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> found in <a href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological" class="mw-redirect">archaeological</a> ruins and often including in statues and architecture, inspired by the mythology of the creature. Cerberus' depiction in ancient art is not as definitive as in literature; the poets and linguists of ancient Greece and Rome mostly agreed on the physical appearance (with the notable exception in <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Theogony" title="Theogony">Theogony</a></i> in which he had 50 heads).<sup id="cite_ref-hesiod-differs_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hesiod-differs-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> His depiction in classical art mostly shows the recurring motif of serpents, but the number of heads differs.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> A statue in the <a href="/wiki/Galleria_Borghese" title="Galleria Borghese">Galleria Borghese</a> depicts Cerberus with three heads sitting by the side of Hades, while a bronze sculpture depicting Heracles' twelfth labour shows the demi-god leading a two-headed Cerberus from the underworld. The majority of vases depicting the twelfth task also show Cerberus as having two heads.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Classical critics have identified one of the earliest works of Cerberus as "the most imaginative," that being a Laconian vase created around 560 BC in which Cerberus is shown with three-heads and with rows of serpents covering his body and heads.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerberus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "Cerberus" is a <a href="/wiki/Latinised" title="Latinised" class="mw-redirect">Latinised</a> version of the Greek <i>Kerberos</i>. The etymology of this name prior to Greek is disputed. It has been claimed to be related to the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> word सर्वरा <i>sarvarā</i>, used as an epithet of one of the dogs of <a href="/wiki/Yama" title="Yama">Yama</a>, from a <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> word *<i>k̑érberos</i>, meaning "spotted"<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> (This etymology suffers from the fact that it includes a reconstructed *<i>b</i>, which is extremely rare in Proto-Indo-European. Yet according to <a href="/wiki/Julius_Pokorny" title="Julius Pokorny">Julius Pokorny</a> it is well distributed, with additional apparent cognates in Slavic, British and Lithuanian.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup>) The use of a dog is uncertain,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> although mythologists have speculated that the association was first made in the city of Trikarenos in Phliasia.<sup id="cite_ref-maurice-7_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maurice-7-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Bruce Lincoln (1991),<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> among others, critiques this etymology; and Ogden (2013)<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> refers to it as "dismissed".</p>
<p>Lincoln notes a similarity between Cerberus and the <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythological</a> dog <a href="/wiki/Garmr" title="Garmr">Garmr</a>, relating both names to a Proto-Indo-European root <i>*ger-</i> "to growl" (perhaps with the suffixes <i>-*m/*b</i> and <i>-*r</i>). However, as Ogden observes, this analysis actually requires <i>Cerberus</i> and <i>Garmr</i> to be derived from two <i>different</i> Indo-European roots (*<i>ger-</i> and *<i>gher-</i> respectively), and so does not actually establish a relationship between the two names.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanations">Explanations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerberus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Explanations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have been many attempts to explain the depiction of Cerberus. A 2nd century CE Greek known as <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus_the_paradoxographer" title="Heraclitus the paradoxographer">Heraclitus the paradoxographer</a> - not to be confused with the 5th century BCE Greek philosopher <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus" title="Heraclitus">Heraclitus</a> - claimed <a href="/wiki/Euhemerism" title="Euhemerism">euhemeristically</a> that Cerberus had two pups that were never away from their father, and that Cerberus was in fact a normal (though very large) dog, but that artists incorporating the two pups into their work made it appear as if his two children were in fact extra heads.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Classical historians have dismissed Heraclitus the paradoxographer's explanation as "feeble".<sup id="cite_ref-maurice-7_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maurice-7-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Mythologers have speculated that if Cerberus were given his name in Trikarenos it could be interpreted as "three karenos".<sup id="cite_ref-maurice-7_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maurice-7-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Certain experts believe that the monster was inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Golden_jackal" title="Golden jackal">golden jackal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GJ_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GJ-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
